## Bibliography

All entries verified against publisher pages / CrossRef in a dedicated pass (2026-06-21). Two corrections from that pass are recorded: the ~80% breakdown figure is Boese et al. (2021), not the V-Dem 2026 report (whose own current-wave figure is ~70%); and an undateable Gailmard working paper on "capacity traps" was dropped in favor of published anchors for the build-slow/destroy-fast asymmetry.

Acemoglu, D., and Robinson, J. A. (2019). *The Narrow Corridor: States, Societies, and the Fate of Liberty*. Penguin Press. — liberty survives only in a narrow corridor balancing state and society; grounds the boundary warning that hardening the state can itself leave the corridor.

Alesina, A., and Tabellini, G. (1990). A positive theory of fiscal deficits and government debt. *Review of Economic Studies*, 57(3), 403–414. — incumbents strategically constrain successors (debt); the formal basis for successor-only rules.

Aubin, J.-P. (1991). *Viability Theory*. Birkhäuser. — keeping a constrained dynamical system inside its viability kernel; the framing of "intervene while inside the safe set."

Basic Law for the Federal Republic of Germany (1949). Article 67 (constructive vote of no confidence). — primary source; remove a chancellor only by electing a successor (removable but not destructible government).

Bermeo, N. (2016). On democratic backsliding. *Journal of Democracy*, 27(1), 5–19. — executive aggrandizement and salami-slicing as the dominant modern mode of backsliding.

Besley, T., and Persson, T. (2009). The origins of state capacity: property rights, taxation, and politics. *American Economic Review*, 99(4), 1218–1244. — state capacity as forward-looking investment under uncertainty about future control; frames K as accumulated investment.

Besley, T., and Persson, T. (2011). *Pillars of Prosperity: The Political Economics of Development Clusters*. Princeton University Press. — book-length treatment of capacity as co-evolving fiscal/legal pillars; the build-slow side of the asymmetry.

Boese, V. A., Edgell, A. B., Hellmeier, S., Maerz, S. F., and Lindberg, S. I. (2021). How democracies prevail: democratic resilience as a two-stage process. *Democratization*, 28(5), 885–907. — once autocratization begins, only ~1 in 5 democracies avert breakdown; the empirical anchor for the drift result.

Fearon, J. D. (2011). Self-enforcing democracy. *Quarterly Journal of Economics*, 126(4), 1661–1708. — elections supply the public, common-knowledge signal that lets citizens coordinate to defend democracy; the lambda-floor argument.

Ginsburg, T., and Huq, A. Z. (2018). *How to Save a Constitutional Democracy*. University of Chicago Press. — distinguishes rapid collapse from slow erosion; guardian-institution design.

Guedes-Neto, J. V., and Peters, B. G. (2025). *Bureaucratic Resistance in Times of Democratic Backsliding*. Cambridge University Press. — survey experiments with 942 officials in the US, UK, Brazil; bureaucratic fidelity is variable and depends on more than formal rules; the destroy-fast (decentralized exit) side.

International IDEA. (2023). *Designing Resistance: Democratic Institutions and the Threat of Backsliding*. International Institute for Democracy and Electoral Assistance. — staggered appointments so guardian bodies embody several majorities across time; the catalogue of capture-resistant design.

Levitsky, S., and Ziblatt, D. (2018). *How Democracies Die*. Crown. — erosion through capture of referees rather than coups; the framing of incremental, legalistic attack.

Maeda, K. (2010). Two modes of democratic breakdown: a competing risks analysis of democratic durability. *Journal of Politics*, 72(4), 1129–1143. — breakdown by external force vs by an elected leader; the competing-risks view behind the drift study.

Ostrom, E. (1990). *Governing the Commons: The Evolution of Institutions for Collective Action*. Cambridge University Press. — institutions for durable collective action; redundancy and nested authority.

Ostrom, V., Tiebout, C. M., and Warren, R. (1961). The organization of government in metropolitan areas: a theoretical inquiry. *American Political Science Review*, 55(4), 831–842. — the foundational polycentricity argument; redundancy raises capture cost only if centers are genuinely independent.

Przeworski, A. (2010). *Democracy and the Limits of Self-Government*. Cambridge University Press. — democracy as self-enforcing equilibrium sustained by the losers' expectation of future competition; the lambda-floor.

V-Dem Institute. (2026). *Democracy Report 2026: Unraveling the Democratic Era?* University of Gothenburg, V-Dem Institute. — 44 countries with ~41% of world population autocratizing; the scale of the current wave. (The ~80% breakdown figure is cited to Boese et al. 2021, not this report.)

Venice Commission. (2002). *Code of Good Practice in Electoral Matters*. Council of Europe. — fundamental electoral rules should not change in the year before an election; the successor-only / slow-rules principle.
